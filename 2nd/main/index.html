Poem

make sure to scroll

In a kingdom, worn and weary, sat old Lear upon his throne, A crown of thorns upon his head, in regal robes, alone. His kingdom, once a beacon bright, now shadows creeping near, For folly's hand had gripped the heart of aging King Lear. Three daughters, like three fates, did weave their father’s tragic lore, Goneril and Regan, hearts of stone, ambition in their core. Cordelia, the youngest, true, her love she did not dress, But simple words, the truth did speak, earned banishment no less.

"Divide my realm," the old king cried, "to she who loves me most, Proclaim your love, and win your share, let flattery be your host." The elder sisters spun their tales, deceit as their design, While Cordelia's silence spoke of love, pure, undressed, divine. A kingdom split, a father fooled by sweetened, poisoned words, Lear’s eyes, by pride and age concealed, saw not the looming swords. Goneril and Regan’s plots unfold, as power was their aim, While Lear, a puppet king, danced in their cruel and heartless game.

The storm did rage, both in his heart and on the heath so wild, A king, now mad, his realm in ruins, lost and beguiled. His fool, his shadow, spoke in riddles, truths that kings despise, Yet in his folly Lear found sense, beneath the tempest skies. A tragedy of errors, his daughters' hearts revealed, Betrayed and broken, left to wander, his fate was sealed. Cordelia’s arms, though open, came too late to mend, The fractured bonds, the kingdom's woes, a family’s bitter end.

Oh, Lear, a king not by wisdom, but by lineage and by name, A father, flawed, whose legacy was naught but grief and shame. In Shakespeare’s tome, a lesson scribed in tears and tempest ink, Beware the hollow flattery that makes a kingdom sink.
